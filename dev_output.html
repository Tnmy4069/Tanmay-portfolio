AI Impact Summit India: The Good, The Bad, and The Shift It Created in Me

DATE: 25/02/2026SLUG: /ai-impact-summit-student-experience

This wasn’t just another tech event. It felt like a signal — that something structural is changing in India’s technology ecosystem.

But like every large-scale event, it had its strengths and its flaws.

Here’s my honest perspective.

The Good: A New India is Emerging

Five years ago, tech exposure meant job fairs and resume drops.

Now, we have AI expos where startups, global companies, policymakers, and students share the same floor.

That shift alone says a lot.

1. Connecting Vision With Execution

While waiting in line early in the day, I met Aditya Chawla, Manager – Global Client Account Management at Avasco.

One line from him changed how I navigated the summit:

“Networking isn’t about meeting everyone. It’s about knowing where to spend your time — and where not to.”

That insight helped me focus on meaningful conversations instead of chasing every stall.

And that’s where the real value of such events lies — alignment between curious engineers and people actually building things.

2. Google: AI Is Becoming Vertical

Google’s stall stood out because it wasn’t generic.

It was divided into domain-specific sections:

  • Agriculture use cases
  • Cross-language solutions
  • Gemini for branding and marketing
  • Notebook LLM
  • Healthcare applications
  • Even a cricket setup using AI to improve gameplay

What impressed me most wasn’t the tech.

It was the depth of knowledge of the staff at each section. They weren’t just explaining features — they understood real-world applications.

AI is no longer horizontal hype. It’s vertical and practical.

3. Meta: Product Reality Over Hype

I tried the Meta Ray-Ban glasses for the first time.

They were good — though not flawless. At times, I had to repeat myself to get a proper response.

That was refreshing.

It reminded me that even global tech giants are iterating in public.

The team there — many of them college students — had solid product knowledge and were genuinely helpful.

4. Sarvam AI: Sovereign AI for India

Before attending, I didn’t know much about Sarvam AI.

After speaking with their team, I understood their positioning clearly:

They are building AI that understands India.

Founded by Dr. Vivek Raghavan and Dr. Pratyush Kumar, Sarvam focuses on sovereign AI infrastructure — developed, deployed, and governed within India.

Their philosophy centers on:

  • AI for India
  • AI for everyday use
  • AI for the community
  • Enabling developers with cost-efficient, high-quality models

This isn’t about importing intelligence. It’s about shaping it.

During the Sarvam launch session, I also briefly met Education Minister Dharmendra Pradhan, reinforcing how AI is no longer just a tech conversation — it’s a policy and nation-building conversation.

5. OpenAI & First Experience With Sora

At the OpenAI stall, I tried Sora for the first time and attempted generating a video.

The demo was fascinating — but short. The stall had to clear shortly after due to a ministerial visit.

Still, it made one thing clear:

Generative media is accelerating faster than most of us realize.

6. Startups, Agri-Tech & Real-World Systems

What truly impressed me was the startup ecosystem presence.

Agri-tech. Healthcare AI. Agentic AI. Industrial applications.

These weren’t just dashboards. These were deployable solutions.

I also had a system-level discussion with Ankur Rastogi from CRIS about how AI is being used to smooth complex railway systems and mitigate operational issues.

That grounded the summit for me.

AI isn’t powerful because it generates text.

It’s powerful when it integrates into national-scale systems.

The Bad: Where Improvement Is Needed

Now for the uncomfortable part.

While the innovations were inspiring, the media narrative wasn’t.

Throughout the summit, instead of discussing breakthrough sessions, billion-dollar ideas, or promising founders, most coverage revolved around a single controversy — a robot dog incident involving a university demonstration.

One isolated moment overshadowed days of serious work and innovation.

The unfortunate reality:

Indian media often amplifies spectacle over substance.

And that does a disservice to the ecosystem.

The Missed Opportunity

The keynote areas were extremely crowded. Many people, including myself, chose to invest time in direct conversations instead of live sessions.

While the summit was clearly organized with strong intent, better crowd management and session accessibility would elevate the experience significantly.

The Bigger Picture

Despite imperfections, the overall direction felt clear.

I saw:

  • School students discovering AI for the first time
  • Engineers exploring new domains
  • Policymakers discussing sovereign infrastructure
  • Startups solving local problems

This is no longer about catching up with the West.

This is about building systems rooted in Indian realities.

What Changed for Me

I went in thinking about AI applications.

I came out thinking about AI systems.

Not just building features — but understanding:

  • Infrastructure
  • Deployment
  • Scalability
  • Domain integration
  • Real-world constraints

The summit didn’t just give information.

It upgraded ambition.

Final Thought

No major ecosystem grows without friction.

Yes, there were flaws. Yes, there were controversies.

But the larger signal was unmistakable:

India is not just consuming AI anymore.

It is beginning to shape it.

And that shift is worth paying attention to.
